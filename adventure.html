Adventure at altitude

Choose altitude-friendly outings first, then push higher once your body agrees.

Day Treks

One day, unforgettable

Peru's day treks punch well above their weight. A single morning can take you from cobblestone streets to glacier lakes at over 4,000 metres. Start early, move at your own pace, and always carry more water than you think you need.

Day trek

Laguna Humantay

  • Emerald glacier lake at 4,200 m; start before 6:00 to avoid clouds.
  • Layers and rain shell essential — weather flips fast.
  • Distance: 8 km round trip · Difficulty: moderate
Half day

Rainbow Mountain

  • Vinicunca's mineral-striped slopes at 5,200 m — one of Peru's most photographed landscapes.
  • Depart Cusco at 4:00 am to reach the summit before afternoon clouds roll in.
  • Distance: 10 km round trip · Difficulty: hard
Easy

Pisac ruins walk

  • Inca terraces and temple complex above the Sacred Valley market town.
  • Combine with the Sunday artisan market at the base for a full day.
  • Distance: 5 km · Difficulty: easy–moderate

Multi-day Treks

The long routes

Peru's multi-day treks are among the finest in the world. Whether you walk the classic Inca Trail or take the wilder Salkantay route, these journeys reward patience with perspectives no day-tripper will ever see. Physical preparation matters — train for elevation gain, not just distance.

Multi‑day

Salkantay Trek

  • Less crowded approach to Machu Picchu with high passes and jungle finish.
  • Plan 4–5 days; book mules locally for lighter packs.
  • Max elevation: 4,600 m · Difficulty: challenging
Classic

Inca Trail

  • The original 4-day route ending at Machu Picchu's Sun Gate at dawn.
  • Permits sell out months in advance — book by January for peak season.
  • Max elevation: 4,215 m · Difficulty: moderate–hard
Remote

Ausangate Circuit

  • 7-day loop around the sacred Ausangate glacier (6,384 m).
  • Passes through high-altitude wetlands with llama and alpaca herds.
  • Max elevation: 5,200 m · Difficulty: very hard

Adrenaline

Push your limits

Beyond trekking, Peru offers world-class adrenaline experiences. From descending one of the deepest canyons on earth to white-water rafting through the Sacred Valley, the country rewards those who venture beyond the standard tourist trail.

Adrenaline

Colca Canyon

  • Descend twice the depth of the Grand Canyon; spot condors on thermals.
  • Hydrate — Arequipa heat sneaks up after chilly canyon mornings.
  • 2-day trek recommended · Base camp at Sangalle oasis
Water

Urubamba River rafting

  • Class III–IV rapids through the Sacred Valley — suitable for beginners with a guide.
  • Half-day tours depart from Cusco daily; equipment provided.
  • Best season: May–November · ~$40–60 USD
Downhill

Mountain biking, Cusco

  • Descend from 4,300 m to 2,800 m on dirt tracks through Andean villages.
  • Full-day guided tours include bike, helmet, and transport back to Cusco.
  • Difficulty: moderate · ~$50–80 USD

Safety

Go prepared, stay safe

Adventure in Peru comes with real altitude risks. Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) can affect anyone — regardless of age or fitness level. The golden rule: ascend slowly, hydrate constantly, and never push through serious symptoms. Descend immediately if you experience severe headache, vomiting, or loss of coordination.

Altitude

Acclimatize in Cusco

  • Spend at least 2 full days in Cusco (3,399 m) before any strenuous activity.
  • Coca tea is a local remedy — widely available and genuinely effective.
Kit

What to carry

  • 2L water minimum, sunscreen SPF 50+, layers, rain shell, headlamp.
  • Trekking poles reduce knee strain by up to 25% on steep descents.
Guides

Always hire locally

  • Licensed local guides carry first aid kits and know evacuation routes.
  • For Inca Trail and Salkantay, a guide is legally required.
